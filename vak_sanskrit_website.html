The Sacred Tongue

A few wonders of the world's most precisely crafted language

❧ ❧ ❧
𑀲

3500+ Years Old

The Rigveda, composed c. 1500 BCE, contains the oldest known Sanskrit texts — hymns so precisely preserved that scholars can still reconstruct the original pronunciation.

व

49 Phonemes

Sanskrit's Devanāgarī script is perfectly phonetic — each symbol maps to exactly one sound. Pāṇini's Ashtādhyāyī described Sanskrit grammar with 3,959 sutras around 400 BCE.

छ

Chandas — The Science of Metre

Over 600 distinct metrical patterns were catalogued by Piṅgala c. 200 BCE. His work, the Chandaśāstra, invented binary notation 2,000 years before Leibniz.

र

Mother of Indo-European

Sanskrit shares deep roots with Greek, Latin, Persian, English and most European languages. "Father" (pitṛ), "mother" (mātr), "name" (nāman) — the echoes reach everywhere.

स

Computer-Ready Grammar

NASA scientists noted in the 1980s that Sanskrit's paninian grammar is the only natural human language suitable for unambiguous machine-language programming.

ध

96 Words for Love

Sanskrit has a staggering vocabulary of emotional nuance — 96 words for love, 67 for water, distinct terms for every shade of beauty, time, and consciousness.

Goddess of knowledge, music, arts, wisdom and learning. She holds the vīṇā and the Vedas, patron of Sanskrit itself.

The scribe of the Mahābhārata — said to have written down Vyāsa's dictation with his own broken tusk as a pen without pause.

The preserver, Nārāyaṇa — around whom swirl 1,000 names (Viṣṇu Sahasranāma), one of the greatest Sanskrit verses ever composed.

What Is Chandas?

Guru · Heavy
A G (Guru) syllable is "heavy" — it contains a long vowel (ā, ī, ū) or is followed by a consonant cluster. It takes 2 mātrās (time units).
Laghu · Light
A L (Laghu) syllable is "light" — it contains a short vowel (a, i, u) not followed by a cluster. It takes 1 mātrā.
Pāda · Foot
A Pāda is a quarter-verse. Most Sanskrit metres have 4 pādas. The Anuṣṭubh śloka (32 syllables, 4×8) is the most widely used metre in all of Sanskrit literature.
Gāyatrī Metre
The sacred Gāyatrī mantra uses the Gāyatrī metre: 3 pādas of 8 syllables each (24 total). It is considered the "mother of all metres" in the Vedas.

Ancient Texts & History

From the primordial hymns of the Ṛgveda to the mathematical elegance of Pāṇini — a journey through 5,000 years

The Śruti — "That Which Is Heard"

The directly revealed scriptures, passed orally for millennia before being written

c. 1500–1200 BCE

Ṛgveda

The oldest religious text in any Indo-European language. 10,552 mantras in 1,028 hymns, composed across 10 maṇḍalas (books). Primarily hymns to Agni (fire), Indra, and Varuṇa. The word "veda" itself means "knowledge."

c. 1200–900 BCE

Sāmaveda & Yajurveda

The Sāmaveda is the "Veda of melodies" — most of its 1,875 verses are drawn from the Ṛgveda but set to musical notation (sāman). It is the direct ancestor of Indian classical music. The Yajurveda contains prose mantras for ritual.

c. 900–700 BCE

Atharvaveda

The fourth Veda — more earthly in focus, containing hymns for healing, longevity, cosmology, marriage, and statecraft. It contains early mathematical and astronomical observations.

c. 800–200 BCE

Upaniṣads (108 texts)

The philosophical culmination of the Vedic period. The word means "to sit near" — as a student sits near a teacher. They introduce the key concepts of Brahman (universal consciousness), Ātman (self), karma, and mokṣa. The Bṛhadāraṇyaka and Chāndogya Upaniṣads are among the oldest philosophical texts in the world.

The Smṛti — "That Which Is Remembered"

The authored epics, purāṇas, and law codes of classical Sanskrit literature

c. 400 BCE – 400 CE

Mahābhārata

The world's longest epic poem — approximately 1.8 million words, roughly ten times the length of the Iliad and Odyssey combined. Attributed to sage Vyāsa, scribed by Gaṇeśa. Contains the Bhagavad Gītā (700 verses), the Viṣṇu Sahasranāma, and encyclopaedic lore spanning dharma, artha, kāma, and mokṣa.

c. 500 BCE – 300 CE

Rāmāyaṇa

24,000 verses in 7 kāṇḍas (books) by Vālmīki — the "first poet" (ādi kavi). The story of Rāma and Sītā is one of the most beloved and widely adapted narratives on earth, influencing the cultures of South and Southeast Asia for three millennia.

c. 300–1200 CE

18 Mahāpurāṇas

The great encyclopaedic texts — the Bhāgavata Purāṇa, Viṣṇu Purāṇa, Śiva Purāṇa, and 15 others. Together they contain cosmology, genealogies, geography, pilgrimage sites, temple architecture, and devotional theology. The Bhāgavata alone runs to 18,000 verses.

c. 400 BCE

Aṣṭādhyāyī by Pāṇini

Perhaps the most extraordinary intellectual achievement of antiquity — a complete, generative grammar of Sanskrit in 3,959 sūtras (rules), so precise and economical that modern linguists consider it a masterpiece of formal linguistics, anticipating Chomskyan generative grammar by 2,400 years.

c. 200 BCE

Chandaśāstra by Piṅgala

The science of Sanskrit metres. In cataloguing syllable patterns, Piṅgala described binary sequences (using laghu = 0, guru = 1) and the Pascal's triangle — making this the earliest known description of binary arithmetic and combinatorics in history.

c. 200 BCE – 200 CE

Arthaśāstra by Kauṭilya

A remarkably sophisticated treatise on statecraft, economic policy, military strategy, and espionage by Candragupta Maurya's minister. Often compared to Machiavelli's Prince, but written 1,800 years earlier, covering everything from foreign policy to market regulation.
